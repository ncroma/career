02Aptoide Connect

Aptoide Connect is Aptoide's platform for app distribution and monetization serving 250M+ users, 20+ integrated stores, and $60M+ in annual transactions. It has two surfaces: a Portal, a public landing page that brings people to the platform; and a Console, the operational system where developers and store partners work. The Portal speaks outward while the Console works inward. I shaped both.

Challenge

Two distinct surfaces, each requiring a different frontend focus:

The Portal

The public-facing entry point for all of Aptoide Connect's services, the first surface developers and app store partners interact with.

This is where I pushed hardest on frontend craft: SEO architecture, performance optimization, mobile-first development, and rigorous cross-browser QA to deliver the best possible front page for the product.

SEO Performance Mobile-first React.js Next.js
The Console

A management platform serving two distinct user types: developers submitting and managing app lifecycles, integrating billing SDKs, and tracking distribution metrics; and app store partners controlling and optimizing the monetization and distribution streams fed by our catalog.

This meant implementing and maintaining features like payment flows, a deeply configurable app distribution layer, wallet and funds management, and a multi-state account system covering distinct user roles, granular permissions, and authentication settings. All fully responsive across desktop and mobile.

Business-critical Flows State Management Responsive Angular RxJS
My Role

Both products I worked on were someone else's starting point. I made them mine. What started as a QA position kept growing in responsibility, breadth, and in the kind of engineer it pushed me to become.

01

QA with a Developer's Eye

I joined as a QA engineer but started building automation from day one. Programming wasn't new to me, and manual testing at scale felt like a problem worth solving properly.

02

Scrum Mastering

I took on the Scrum Master role alongside my technical work ensuring agile practices were followed and all ceremonies ran smoothly, as well as stepping up for product demos and OKRs.

03

The Developer-QA Model

I became the first developer-QA on the team, someone who both develops and tests. It proved its worth and became the standard: everyone develops, everyone tests. The dedicated QA role dissolved into the team, but the testing automation didn't: it became a household tool the team kept maintaining and evolving, and in some form it's still running today.

04

Portal, Console, and the Drive for Quality

I moved into development on the Admin BO first and then on both the Portal and the Console, all in Angular at the time. The Portal shifted to server-side rendering for SEO; the Console went through deep rework to become a platform developers and partners could genuinely trust with their business.

05

Rebrand & Learning React

A product rebrand reshaped both surfaces, most visibly the Portal. Alongside that I picked up React, building independent product and marketing pages that pushed me outside the core Angular codebase.

06

AI, Speed, and Going Full Stack

When AI entered our workflow everything accelerated. That's when my QA background showed its real value: moving fast with AI requires knowing exactly what to verify. The Portal was migrated from Angular to React, unlocking proper SSR (a migration that would have been impractical without AI). My productivity spiked, and the bandwidth that created is where I started crossing into backend territory for the first time, picking up Node.js tasks and taking my first real steps as a full-stack developer.

03Manifesto

The principles that shape how I build, test, and ship software, refined over years of doing all three.

  1. Requirements Are the Floor, Not the Ceiling

    Specs define scope, not quality.

  2. Quality Is Not a Phase

    Quality isn't a phase at the end of development, it's a discipline baked into every step.

  3. AI Accelerates. Judgement Guides.

    The engineers who thrive with AI aren't the ones who generate the most code; they're the ones who own every line they ship.
